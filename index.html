From Shooters to Skills: How Much Do Action Video Games Really Boost Cognition?

Can playing fast-paced shooter games genuinely improve how the brain perceives and processes the world? And if so, does this effect extend beyond habitual players to people who are trained to play? These questions are at the heart of a growing body of research on action video games. The meta-analysis by Bediou and colleagues (2023), "Effects of Action Video Game Play on Cognitive Skills: A Meta-Analysis," synthesises over two decades of studies to give the most comprehensive answer yet.Read on to find out what the evidence says — and how much confidence we can place in it.

Introduction

Video games are often treated as one homogeneous category, but Bediou et al. argue that this is like lumping all food together when studying diet: some games strongly tax particular mental skills, others barely do. The focus here is on action video games (AVGs), meaning First‑person shooters (FPS) and Third‑person shooters (TPS) because they are characterised by many fast‑moving targets, cluttered visual scenes and rapid decision‑making under time pressure. AVGs in general heavily load core perceptual and cognitive functions

This has led to the hypothesis that playing such games essentially trains these functions, and that this training can at least somewhat generalize to tasks outside of games. Prior meta‑analyses already pointed to a positive link between gaming and cognition, but the data has grown in the last years and there were some methodological issues in past analyses. Therefore Bediou et al (2023) conducted a new meta analysis, seperating cross-sectional studies and intervention-studies.

What is this study about?

The meta-analysis by Bediou and colleagues (2023) examines two related questions simultaneously. The first is whether people who habitually play action video games differ systematically from non-players in their cognitive abilities. This is addressed through a meta-analysis of 105 cross-sectional studies (221 effect sizes), in which researchers compared groups of experienced action game players with individuals who played little or no video games. The second question is whether playing action games causally produces cognitive improvements, which is addressed through a separate meta-analysis of 28 intervention studies (91 effect sizes), in which non-players were randomly assigned to train on an action game or an active control game for a minimum of eight hours spread across at least eight sessions.

Only first- and third-person shooter games qualified as action video games for this study. Control games included puzzle games, life simulations, turn-based strategy games, and similar genres that do not share the core mechanics of action games. Brain-training games were explicitly excluded from the control condition, since they are designed to improve cognition and would confound the comparison. Studies conducted on mobile devices were also excluded, as small screens prevent the divided attention load that characterises action game play.

Across both study designs, the authors focused on eight cognitive domanis:

Perception Precision or speed of perceptual information processing perceptual discrimination, contrast sensitivity

What does this study find?

Cross-sectional results: players vs. non-players

In the cross-sectional meta-analysis, the overall advantage of action game players over non-players was large (g = 0.64). The strongest differences appeared in perception, visuospatial cognition, multitasking, and top-down attention — the domains that action games most directly demand. Inhibition, verbal cognition, and problem-solving showed moderate effects. Bottom-up attention was the only domain that did not reach significance, which the authors interpret as consistent with the idea that automatic, reflexive attention is phylogenetically ancient, driven substantially by subcortical structures, and therefore less plastic in adulthood than goal-driven attentional control.

Intervention results: action-game trainig vs. non-action-game training

In the intervention meta-analysis, the causal effect of action game training was smaller but still significant (g = 0.30). Top-down attention was the domain most clearly and consistently improved by training. Verbal cognition showed essentially no benefit (g = 0.01). Longer training durations were associated with larger effects overall, though this relationship was complicated by the fact that studies with the longest durations tended to measure perception specifically, making it difficult to separate the effect of duration from the effect of the domain being trained.

Across different cognitive domains, the effect sizes looked like this:

Cognitive domain g
Perception 0.23
Bottom-up attention 0.19
Top-down attention 0.52*
Spatial cognition 0.26
Multitasking 0.41
Verbal cognition 0.01

Importantly, both analyses revealed substantial heterogeneity: the average effect sizes summarise a wide distribution of individual study results, and the moderator analyses did not identify statistically significant sources of this variation. This means the field cannot yet reliably predict which conditions produce larger or smaller benefits.

How substantial are the effects?

The cross-sectional effect of g = 0.64 is large by conventional standards, meaning that on average, action game players score considerably higher on cognitive tasks than non-players. However, this figure must be interpreted with caution: publication bias was detected in the cross-sectional data, meaning that studies with null or negative results may have been underrepresented. After correcting for this bias using the three-parameter selection model, the estimated true effect was g = 0.48, which is still meaningful, but notably smaller. The intervention effect of g = 0.30 is small by conventional standardsand was not subject to detectable publication bias, which lends it greater credibility.

How differentiated is the presentation of results?

The meta-analysis goes considerably beyond a single overall effect by distinguishing nine cognitive domains, two study designs, and several moderators including training duration, recruitment method, and the type of dependent measure used. This differentiation reveals an important pattern: not all cognitive abilities are equally responsive to action game play. The clearest and most robust causal benefits concentrate in top-down attention — the ability to deliberately direct and sustain attention toward goal-relevant information. Verbal cognition, by contrast, appears essentially unaffected by training, which makes theoretical sense given that action games place no meaningful load on verbal storage or rehearsal. The authors also distinguish between effects measured via accuracy and those measured via speed, finding comparable effect sizes for both — an important finding that rules out the possibility that action game players are simply trading accuracy for faster responses.

How generalisable are the findings?

The intervention findings are restricted to healthy young adults aged 18–45, and all studies used commercially available first- and third-person shooters as the training game. Whether similar effects would be found in children, older adults, or with games designed specifically to train cognition remains untested in this literature. The cross-sectional findings are also limited by the changing landscape of gaming: as the authors acknowledge, the population of genuine non-gamers has shrunk dramatically over the past two decades, making it increasingly difficult to construct a meaningful comparison group. Studies conducted in the early 2000s may therefore not be directly comparable to more recent work, and the average cross-sectional effect may have decreased over time for reasons unrelated to the games themselves.

What makes this meta-analysis scientifically relevant?

This meta-analysis is the most methodologically rigorous synthesis of the action video game literature to date. It is pre-registered, the full dataset and analysis code are publicly available, and it uses modern statistical techniques that appropriately model the hierarchical and correlated structure of the data, which is a clear improvement over earlier meta-analyses in this area, including the authors' own previous work. The separation of cross-sectional and intervention studies into distinct analyses is also important: by treating these as separate questions requiring separate answers, the authors avoid conflating evidence about associations with evidence about causal effects. The active control design required of all intervention studies is equally valuable, ensuring that any observed gains reflect something specific to action games rather than to gaming in general.

Conclusion for Practice

The meta-analysis by Bediou and colleagues (2023) provides the strongest available evidence that action video game play is genuinely associated with, and to a modest degree causally responsible for, improvements in certain cognitive skills, particularly top-down attention and, to a lesser extent, perception and visuospatial cognition. The intervention effects are real but small, and they concentrate in abilities that action games directly demand. Verbal memory, inhibition, and problem-solving appear to benefit little if at all.

For applied settings like as cognitive training programmes, rehabilitation, or educational contexts, these findings suggest that action game training may be a viable and engaging tool for improving attentional control, but should not be expected to produce broad, general cognitive enhancement. The practical significance of a g = 0.30 effect depends heavily on the context: for domains where even small improvements in attentional capacity matter the effect may well be meaningful. For general educational purposes, however, the evidence does not yet support strong claims. Future research should focus on identifying which specific game mechanics drive the observed benefits, testing effects in populations beyond healthy young adults, and measuring outcomes over longer follow-up periods to assess whether training-induced gains are sustained.
